# 10. Language Architecture

## Purpose

Language Architecture defines how ApexCrest chooses words.

It exists to prevent the voice from becoming vague, over-poetic, franchise-coded, storage-led or inconsistent.

The language system must protect four things:

1. ApexCrest as a premium illustrated collector book.
2. The emotional-world system.
3. The collector's freedom to choose meaning.
4. Legal and commercial distance from existing TCG brands.

## Core language structure

ApexCrest language is organised into four tiers:

1. **Canonical vocabulary** — words ApexCrest may use confidently.
2. **Careful vocabulary** — useful words that require context and restraint.
3. **Avoid vocabulary** — words that create legal, tonal, franchise or VAT-positioning risk.
4. **Functional vocabulary** — plain terms needed for clarity, compatibility and customer support.

## How the tiers work

Canonical vocabulary defines the voice.

Careful vocabulary adds range.

Avoid vocabulary protects the brand.

Functional vocabulary keeps the product understandable.

No single piece of copy should rely only on careful or functional vocabulary. It should be anchored in canonical vocabulary first.

## Core language axis

ApexCrest should sit between two poles:

- **Collector truth** — cards, collections, favourites, rarity, artwork, meaning.
- **Book-world language** — worlds, pages, place, story, memory, light, stillness, ascent, sanctuary.

If copy loses collector truth, it becomes fantasy prose.

If copy loses book-world language, it becomes storage or marketplace copy.

The strongest ApexCrest voice holds both together.

## Language hierarchy

When choosing between possible words, use this priority order:

1. Meaning
2. World
3. Book
4. Collector
5. Card
6. Function
7. Compatibility

This order matters.

If compatibility or function leads too often, the product begins to sound like an accessory.

If world language leads without meaning, the product begins to sound like a fantasy setting.

If meaning leads, the language remains ApexCrest.

## Language density rule

ApexCrest copy should avoid overloading a sentence with too many branded, poetic or mythic terms.

One strong image is better than three decorative ones.

Weak:

> Enter a radiant sanctuary of eternal legacy where your treasured cards ascend into myth.

ApexCrest:

> Choose the world that feels right.

## Language clarity rule

Every emotionally rich line must still be understandable on first reading.

If a sentence sounds beautiful but requires explanation, rewrite it.

# 11. Canonical Vocabulary

## Purpose

Canonical vocabulary contains the words ApexCrest should use confidently and repeatedly.

These words are central to the brand's voice and should appear naturally across books, packaging, website, product pages, social media and campaigns.

They are not all exclusive to ApexCrest, but in combination they create an ownable language system.

## Tier 1 — Core ApexCrest vocabulary

Use these confidently.

- ApexCrest
- Crest
- Crested
- Cresting
- Crest-worthy
- world
- place
- earned
- meaning
- card
- collection
- collector
- chosen
- kept
- story
- memory
- wonder
- beauty
- legacy
- honour

## Tier 1 usage principles

### ApexCrest

Use ApexCrest as the brand name, not as a generic noun.

Strong:

> ApexCrest is an illustrated collector book for the cards that earned their place.

Weak:

> Buy an ApexCrest for every card type.

### Crest / Crested / Cresting

Use Crest language to describe the act of giving a card a recognised place.

Do not use it so often that it sounds like a gimmick or mechanical feature.

Strong:

> Not every card needs to be Crested.

Weak:

> Crest your Crested cards into the Cresting system.

### World

Use world as the primary alternative to slot, section or category.

Strong:

> Choose the world that feels right.

Weak:

> Choose the slot that matches your card.

### Place

Use place to express meaning, recognition and belonging.

Strong:

> A place for the cards that earned one.

Weak:

> A place to store your cards.

### Earned

Use earned to express selectivity, not elitism.

Strong:

> For the cards that earned their place.

Weak:

> Only expensive cards have earned this.

### Meaning

Use meaning as the anchor word for emotional value.

Strong:

> Meaning matters more than price.

Weak:

> Add emotional meaning to any card.

Meaning is recognised, not artificially added.

### Chosen

Use chosen to reinforce freedom and selectivity.

Strong:

> Ten worlds. Ten chosen cards.

Weak:

> Ten required cards.

### Kept

Use kept to express care, memory and preservation.

Strong:

> Some cards are kept for reasons only the collector understands.

Weak:

> Keep your cards safely in our holder.

### Story

Use story lightly.

The card may have a story, but the product should not force one.

Strong:

> Every collection carries its own stories.

Weak:

> Write the story of your epic card adventure.

### Memory

Use memory for personal attachment and nostalgia.

Strong:

> Some cards carry a memory before they carry a value.

Weak:

> Memory cards belong in the Forest page.

### Wonder

Use wonder for emotional openness, not fantasy spectacle.

Strong:

> A world built for wonder.

Weak:

> Unlock magical wonder.

### Beauty

Use beauty to recognise artwork, atmosphere and emotional fit.

Strong:

> Some cards are chosen because they are simply beautiful.

Weak:

> Beautify your trading card storage.

### Legacy

Use legacy for long-term significance.

Strong:

> A book made to outlast the moment of the pull.

Weak:

> Build your ultimate legacy empire.

### Honour

Use honour carefully but confidently.

Honour means recognition and respect, not ceremony for its own sake.

Strong:

> Honour the cards that mean the most.

Weak:

> Enter the honour chamber of legendary champions.

## Tier 1 density guidance

A single sentence should usually contain no more than two or three core ApexCrest terms.

Strong:

> Choose the world that feels right.

Weak:

> Crest your chosen card into a world of meaning, memory, beauty and legacy.

# 12. Vocabulary to Use Carefully

## Purpose

Careful vocabulary contains useful words that can strengthen ApexCrest copy when handled with restraint.

These words are not banned. They are context-sensitive.

They can become too franchise-coded, too hype-driven, too fantasy-novel, too competitive or too storage-led if overused.

## Controlled vocabulary list

Use carefully:

- legendary
- adventure
- treasure
- iconic
- power
- triumph
- archive
- vault
- dream
- companion
- transformation
- quest
- magic
- enchanted
- hero
- champion
- rarity
- pull
- chase
- display
- slot
- insert
- mount

## Controlled usage rules

### Legendary

Use only in ordinary descriptive sense, not as a franchise-style power category.

Safer alternatives:

- unforgettable
- rare
- exceptional
- earned
- meaningful

Use:

> A card can feel legendary without being the rarest card you own.

Avoid:

> Crest your legendary champions.

### Adventure

Use sparingly.

It can be useful for broader campaign copy, but it can easily drift toward One Piece, Lorcana or generic fantasy language.

Safer alternatives:

- journey
- path
- discovery
- world

Use:

> A collector's journey changes over time.

Avoid:

> Begin your card adventure today.

### Treasure

Use as a verb or emotional idea more than a noun.

Safer alternatives:

- treasured
- kept
- valued
- meaningful

Use:

> Some cards are treasured for reasons only the collector knows.

Avoid:

> Fill your book with treasure.

### Iconic

Use for cards that are widely recognised or personally defining.

Avoid using it as a generic upgrade word.

Use:

> Some cards become iconic within a collection.

Avoid:

> Make every card iconic.

### Power

Use only when emotional intensity is intended.

Power should not dominate the overall brand voice.

Use:

> Ember is for cards with presence and force.

Avoid:

> Show your most powerful cards and dominate your collection.

### Triumph

Use mainly around Summit and achievement.

Avoid making the brand competitive.

Use:

> Summit carries the feeling of arrival.

Avoid:

> Prove your triumph over other collectors.

### Archive / Vault

Use with caution.

Archive can sound too institutional. Vault can sound storage-led or too close to competitor/product-category language.

Safer alternatives:

- book
- collection
- world
- kept
- preserved

Use:

> A book for the cards worth keeping.

Avoid:

> Store your cards in the ultimate collector vault.

### Dream

Use lightly.

Dream can become Disney/Lorcana-coded or vague.

Use:

> Prism should feel soft, bright and dreamlike.

Avoid:

> Your dream collection awaits.

### Companion

Use rarely.

It may fit collector attachment, but can sound franchise-coded in some contexts.

Use:

> Some cards become companions to a collection over time.

Avoid:

> Find your card companion.

### Transformation

Use conceptually, not as a game/franchise mechanic.

Safer alternatives:

- change
- grow
- become
- shift

Use:

> A card's meaning can change as the collection changes.

Avoid:

> Transform and evolve your cards.

### Quest

Generally avoid unless deliberately used in a limited campaign context.

It often sounds too game-like.

Preferred alternatives:

- choice
- path
- journey
- search

### Magic / Enchanted

Use only in generic prose with extreme caution.

These words carry heavy franchise and category associations.

Preferred alternatives:

- wonder
- light
- beauty
- atmosphere
- feeling

### Hero / Champion

Avoid as general brand language.

These words can become competitive, RPG-like or franchise-coded.

Preferred alternatives:

- chosen card
- meaningful card
- card that earned its place

### Rarity / Pull / Chase

These are collector-native and useful, but should not dominate.

They belong mostly in practical, product-page or social contexts.

Use:

> A card can be chosen for rarity, artwork, memory or instinct.

Avoid:

> Built for your biggest pulls and chase cards.

### Display / Slot / Insert / Mount

These words may be necessary for clarity, but they should not lead brand copy.

Use them in functional or support contexts.

Avoid them in emotional headlines, back-cover copy and in-book editorial text.

# 13. Vocabulary to Avoid

## Purpose

Avoid vocabulary protects ApexCrest from sounding legally risky, franchise-coded, mechanically narrow, storage-led or tonally weak.

These words are not always illegal to mention in all contexts. The rule is that they must not become ApexCrest brand language.

## Franchise-coded terms to avoid

Do not use these as ApexCrest identity language:

- trainer
- catch
- catch them all
- Poké-style compounds
- mana
- planeswalker
- spellbook
- spellcasting
- glimmer
- illumineer
- enchanted as Lorcana-style rarity language
- duel
- duelist
- summon
- monster as a Yu-Gi-Oh-style category
- tamer
- digital world
- Saiyan
- Ki
- living legend
- world of Rathe
- Opus
- set sail
- crew as One Piece-style identity language

## Why these are avoided

They can create one or more risks:

- sounding like a specific TCG
- implying affiliation
- drifting into official-franchise language
- making the product feel like an unofficial expansion
- weakening ApexCrest's own language system

## Slogan-adjacent language to avoid

Avoid lines that echo official or culturally recognisable franchise slogans.

Avoid:

> Catch your favourites.

Avoid:

> Your dream collection awaits.

Avoid:

> Step into the arena.

Avoid:

> Set sail for your collection.

Avoid:

> Choose your hero.

## Storage-first language to avoid in brand copy

Avoid leading with:

- storage
- binder
- album
- holder
- card holder
- display accessory
- mounting system
- slots
- sleeves
- pockets
- insert cards
- fill the book
- complete every space

These words may appear only when necessary for functional clarity, customer support or compatibility explanation.

## Hype language to avoid

Avoid:

- ultimate
- must-have
- game-changing
- revolutionary
- insane
- epic drop
- level up your collection
- the future of collecting
- dominate your collection

ApexCrest should not need exaggerated claims.

## Over-poetic language to avoid

Avoid:

- relic
- eternal vault
- ascended treasure
- sacred chamber
- mythical archive
- destiny-bound card
- legendary realm of remembrance

These push the voice toward fantasy novel, fake luxury or parody.

## Corporate language to avoid

Avoid:

- solution
- product ecosystem
- premium user experience
- value proposition
- customer journey, unless internal
- maximise display potential
- collection optimisation

ApexCrest should sound crafted, not corporate.

# 14. Sentence Rhythm & Cadence

## Purpose

Cadence controls how ApexCrest writing feels when read.

The voice should be calm, clear and emotionally precise.

It should not rush, shout or over-explain.

## Core cadence

ApexCrest uses a four-beat rhythm:

1. A short emotional observation.
2. A sentence that expands the truth.
3. A sentence that connects the truth to the book, world or card.
4. A quiet closing line.

## Four-beat example

> Some cards stay with us.  
> Not because they are the rarest, but because they found us at the right time.  
> ApexCrest gives those cards a world of their own.  
> For the cards that earned their place.

## Short-line rule

Short lines create confidence.

Use them for emphasis, but do not overuse them.

Strong:

> Not every card needs to be Crested.

Weak:

> Not every card.  
> Not every pull.  
> Not every rare.  
> Only destiny.

The weak version becomes melodramatic.

## Sentence-length balance

ApexCrest copy should usually mix:

- one short sentence
- one medium sentence
- one short closing sentence

Avoid long chains of poetic clauses.

Weak:

> Across the shifting landscapes of memory, rarity, beauty and long-held collector meaning, every chosen card begins to discover the emotional place it has always quietly deserved.

ApexCrest:

> Some cards feel different from the moment you keep them.

## Repetition rule

Repetition can be powerful, but it must feel deliberate.

Use repetition for:

- meaning
- contrast
- rhythm
- memorable lines

Avoid repetition of branded terms.

Weak:

> Crest your Crest-worthy card into a Crested world.

ApexCrest:

> Choose the card that earned its place.

## Question cadence

Questions work well in social and product-page copy.

Use questions that invite recognition, not pressure.

Strong:

> Which world would your favourite card choose?

Weak:

> Have you completed all ten slots yet?

## Closing-line cadence

ApexCrest closing lines should feel settled, not sales-driven.

Strong:

> For the cards that earned their place.

Strong:

> A world for the card that stayed with you.

Weak:

> Order now before this premium collector experience sells out.

# 15. Grammar & Style Rules

## Purpose

Grammar and style rules keep ApexCrest clear, consistent and premium across channels.

## English variant

Use UK English by default.

Use:

- honour
- favourite
- colour
- centre
- recognised
- organised

Use US spelling only where required by marketplace fields, quoted product names or external compatibility terms.

## Capitalisation

Use standard sentence case for most headings and copy.

Capitalise:

- ApexCrest
- Crest when used as a branded verb/noun
- Crested
- Crest-worthy
- world names, such as Ember Tribute or Celestial Wonder
- official product names where legally appropriate

Avoid excessive fantasy capitalisation.

Weak:

> Enter the World of Eternal Memory and Choose the Card of Destiny.

ApexCrest:

> Choose the world that feels right.

## Punctuation

Use restrained punctuation.

Avoid frequent exclamation marks.

Avoid ellipses unless deliberately creating pause in a script.

Avoid emoji in core brand copy.

Social media may use light punctuation variation, but the brand should remain composed.

## Contractions

Use contractions sparingly.

They can make founder content and customer support warmer, but in-book and packaging copy should usually be more composed.

Use:

> It is not about the rarest card.

Rather than:

> It isn't about the rarest card.

In customer support, either is acceptable if clarity improves.

## Numbers

Use numerals for product architecture:

- 10 worlds
- 10 chosen cards
- 64 pages

Use words for poetic or editorial emphasis only when needed.

## Lists

Use lists for clarity in product, support and governance copy.

Avoid making in-book text feel like a checklist.

## Jargon

Avoid gameplay jargon unless required for compatibility or customer support.

Avoid collector slang in core brand copy.

Use collector-native words that remain widely understandable.

## Legal disclaimers

Legal disclaimers should be plain and separate from emotional brand copy.

Do not blend disclaimers into poetic language.

## VAT-conscious style note

Do not describe ApexCrest primarily as a holder, binder, album, storage system or display accessory.

When functional language is required, keep it secondary to the book-first description.

## Final style rule

If a sentence sounds clever but less clear, simplify it.

If it sounds premium but less human, warm it.

If it sounds emotional but less true, cut it.

# Phase 2B Language Engine Audit

## Audit status

PASS — with two watch points.

## 1. Does Phase 2B control the `lightly mythic` risk identified in Phase 2A?

Pass.

The guide now clearly states that mythic language is seasoning, not structure. It bans over-poetic and fantasy-novel language and provides safer alternatives.

## 2. Does the language engine protect ApexCrest from franchise imitation?

Pass.

The avoid vocabulary explicitly excludes franchise-coded terminology, slogan-adjacent language and official role/mechanic terms.

## 3. Does it preserve collector authenticity?

Pass.

The canonical and careful vocabulary include collector-native terms such as card, collection, collector, favourite, rarity, pull, chase, artwork and memory.

## 4. Does it remain book-first and VAT-conscious?

Pass.

The language hierarchy prioritises meaning, world and book before function or compatibility. Storage-first terms are restricted to functional contexts.

This is not tax advice, but the wording strategy remains aligned with the current book-first product positioning.

## 5. Does it support scalable content production?

Pass.

The tiered vocabulary system, cadence rules and rewrite logic give future writers repeatable controls.

## 6. Does it remain mass-market clear?

Pass.

The grammar and style rules enforce plain English, restrained punctuation and clarity over cleverness.

## Watch point 1

Some controlled words, such as `legendary`, `treasure`, `dream`, `magic` and `enchanted`, remain commercially tempting because they feel natural in the TCG category.

Mitigation:

They must remain in controlled vocabulary and should be reviewed carefully in public-facing copy.

## Watch point 2

The words `slot`, `insert`, `mount` and `display` may be necessary for customer clarity, but they can weaken the book-first positioning if overused in marketplace copy.

Mitigation:

Phase 2C channel rules must clearly separate emotional copy from functional clarification.

## Phase 2B recommendation

Phase 2B is strong enough to proceed to Phase 2C — Channel Rules.

Do not merge until the PR has been reviewed and approved.
